# GAME IDENTITY — TableRush

**Status:** DESIGN DOCUMENT — no implementation

## 1. WHY CHOOSE TABLERRUSH

Four restaurant games exist. All four are good. Why would a player choose this one?

---

### vs. Diner Dash

Diner Dash is a franchise. It has a story, a protagonist, a growing cast of characters, full music, and the accumulated legacy of two decades of sequels. A player who loves Diner Dash loves Diner Dash specifically. TableRush cannot and should not try to be Diner Dash.

**Where TableRush wins:**

Diner Dash makes you a restaurant owner who also happens to serve. TableRush makes you purely a waiter. No construction, no management, no franchise. Only the floor. The difference is the difference between playing an RTS and playing an FPS — one is strategy, the other is presence.

Diner Dash levels require five to fifteen minutes. TableRush is three. The format difference is not a weakness — it is the product. TableRush is the game you play between subway stops, in a waiting room, during a commercial break. It requires nothing except your attention for three minutes. Diner Dash requires setup, context, and memory of where you left off.

Diner Dash was designed for mouse and keyboard. TableRush was designed for thumbs.

**The honest answer:** Someone chooses TableRush when they want the core feeling of restaurant service — urgency, multitasking, satisfaction — without the overhead of a franchise game. They want the drug, not the narrative around the drug.

---

### vs. Overcooked

Overcooked is a party game designed for two to four players on a couch or screen share. Its solo mode is underserved — the game was not designed to be played alone. Its chaos is meant to be shared.

TableRush is designed for one player. The solo experience is the complete experience, not a diminished version. In Overcooked you coordinate with others. In TableRush you achieve mastery alone. These create completely different emotional experiences.

Overcooked is about the comedy of failure — things going wrong together is funny and memorable. TableRush is about the satisfaction of precision — things going right because you are skilled is proud and motivating.

**The honest answer:** Someone chooses TableRush when they are alone and want to feel competent, not when they are with friends and want to laugh at shared chaos.

---

### vs. PlateUp!

PlateUp! is a restaurant builder and service simulator. A session is thirty to sixty minutes of construction, then service. It is deep, strategic, and systems-rich. It is primarily a PC game with no mobile presence.

TableRush occupies a completely different category. PlateUp! is for players who want to spend an evening building and running a restaurant. TableRush is for players who have three minutes and want to feel something.

**The honest answer:** PlateUp! and TableRush do not compete. They serve different players at different moments. A PlateUp! player might also love TableRush for commute sessions. There is no overlap problem.

---

### vs. Cook Serve Delicious

Cook Serve Delicious puts the player in the kitchen. The challenge is recipe execution — knowing the correct sequence of inputs for each dish, under time pressure. The fantasy is cooking mastery.

TableRush puts the player on the floor. The challenge is service orchestration — reading which table needs attention, moving efficiently, managing multiple simultaneous demands. The fantasy is waiter mastery.

Cook Serve Delicious rewards memorization. TableRush rewards spatial awareness and priority judgment.

**The honest answer:** Someone chooses TableRush when they want to feel like they are reading a room, not following a recipe. The kitchen is not interesting to them — the people are.

---

### The Honest Competitive Summary

TableRush's unique position in one sentence: **The only game that makes you feel like the world's best waiter — completely solo, in exactly three minutes.**

No competitor offers this combination. Not the solo depth. Not the specific perspective (front-of-house, not kitchen, not management). Not the three-minute format. This is TableRush's territory and it belongs to no one else.

## 2. THE UNIQUE FANTASY

**Not the mechanics. The fantasy.**

The mechanics are: tap table, walk, tap kitchen, walk, deliver, collect, clean. Those are actions. They say nothing about why a person keeps playing.

The fantasy is what the player is living out when they tap those things.

---

Here is what the player is actually feeling when TableRush is working:

You enter a busy restaurant. You see everything at once — the couple at table 2 who just finished eating, the impatient businessman at table 4 who has been waiting too long, the kitchen light that just went orange meaning someone's order is ready. You feel the weight of all of it simultaneously, and instead of panicking, you just start moving. Table 4 first — he's the most impatient. Then kitchen. Then table 2.

You don't think. You just know.

And as you deliver the businessman's pasta, he exhales and his posture relaxes. And you're already turning toward the kitchen before he says thank you.

**That is the fantasy.**

Not survival. Not management. Not building. Not cooking.

**Grace under pressure.**

The feeling of being so skilled at something that the hardest situations look easy because you are doing them. The feeling that your hands know where to go before your mind does. The feeling that you are not reacting to the chaos — you are ahead of it.

This is the specific experience that no other restaurant game delivers. Diner Dash makes you a planner. Overcooked makes you a comedian. Cook Serve Delicious makes you a chef.

TableRush makes you a **performer**.

The restaurant is the stage. The customers are the audience. And for three minutes, you are exactly as good as you have always believed you could be.

**The unique fantasy:** *Being the most capable person in the room, and making it look effortless.*

## 5. TWENTY MEMORABLE MOMENTS

These are the specific moments the game exists to create. If a player experiences three of these in a session, they will return.

1. **The one-second save.** A customer's patience bar is at 2%. The delivery animation completes with 1 second to spare. The customer's face changes from angry to relieved. The combo lives.

2. **The silent combo crescendo.** The player realizes, mid-round, that they are at ×3.0 TABLE MASTER. They did not notice it building. They are just performing at their best and the score reflects it.

3. **The three-table clear.** Three tables request simultaneously. The player navigates the priority order perfectly and all three customers receive their orders within seconds of each other. The restaurant is briefly empty. Silence.

4. **The impossible save.** A customer is literally walking toward the exit — the departure animation has begun. The player delivers the food in time and the customer turns around and sits back down.

5. **The personal best that comes from nowhere.** Playing without expectation. No specific goal. The round ends and the score is 400 points above their previous best. How did that happen?

6. **The combo break at ×2.5.** One wrong priority choice. The wrong table, the wrong moment. The combo drops to ×1.0. The visual resets. The player stares at the screen and replays the last 10 seconds in their head.

7. **The kitchen moment.** The player is serving a table across the restaurant when their peripheral attention detects the kitchen is ready. They finish the delivery and pivot directly to the kitchen without stopping — it feels like instinct.

8. **The full house.** All five tables occupied simultaneously. The player is managing five states at once. Three minutes of this would be overwhelming. Ten seconds of this, while everything is under control, feels like being a conductor.

9. **The payment chain.** Three customers finish eating at nearly the same time. The player collects three payments in sequence without a break. Three tip bursts. Three score pops. The score counter jumps.

10. **The dirty table scramble.** A new customer has arrived but all five tables are occupied or dirty. The player cleans at maximum speed and the customer sits down just before their patience for waiting would have expired.

11. **The discovery of the speed multiplier.** For the first time, the player delivers so fast that "⚡ LIGHTNING!" appears. They didn't know that was possible. Immediately they start trying to do it again.

12. **The last 30 seconds.** The warning appears. The player has two dirty tables, one order in the kitchen, and a customer paying. The mental math plays out in 2 seconds. They decide an order. They execute.

13. **The unbroken chain.** Six consecutive happy customers. The combo at ×2.5. The last customer is served, pays, and leaves smiling. Seven. The player does not breathe.

14. **The wrong priority regret.** The player went to the wrong table. They watched the right table lose its patience and the customer leave in real time. They knew, one second before it happened, that they had made the wrong choice. The score drops. The game continues.

15. **The first time reaching TABLE MASTER.** ×3.0. The milestone text. The waiter's excited animation. The realization that every action for the rest of the round is worth triple. Everything changes. This is what the game feels like at its peak.

16. **The 0 angry customers.** The round ends. The end screen shows: angry customers — 0. Perfect service. Every customer served. Nobody left without being taken care of.

17. **The Rush Hour arrival.** A warning appears — "LUNCH RUSH IN 15 SECONDS." Three tables empty. The player cleans them fast. The wave arrives. Six customers enter in eight seconds. The restaurant fills. The player is in the eye of it.

18. **The clock at 0:01.** The timer reaches one second. The player is mid-delivery. The animation completes. The timer hits zero. The session ends with the food in the customer's hands.

19. **The score that doesn't make sense.** The player looks at their score mid-round and cannot believe how high it is. The combo multiplier was doing more than they realized. They change their play immediately to protect it.

20. **The round after a perfect round.** Trying to replicate perfection. Knowing exactly what to do. Failing anyway. The game is harder than perfect looks.

## 6. EMOTIONAL ARC OF A SESSION

---

### Beginning (minute 0:00 – 0:45)

**Target emotion: Calm readiness**

The restaurant is empty. The timer starts. The waiter stands in the center. The first customer approaches from the entrance — their walk animation alone communicates "someone needs something."

The player is not afraid. They know what to do. They tap the table before the customer has fully arrived. They feel the competence of familiarity.

The first delivery completes. The score increases. This is confirmation: the hands still know.

The emotional state at 45 seconds: unhurried confidence. Everything is under control. The player knows this feeling — it is the feeling before the interesting part begins.

---

### Middle (minute 0:45 – 2:00)

**Target emotion: Engaged concentration → Flow**

Two, then three, then four customers are seated. The player is no longer choosing to act — they are reacting to priorities. The mental queue builds: kitchen ready → deliver to table 2 → then table 4 is requesting → then table 1 is about to pay.

Mistakes begin to be possible here. A wrong route choice has consequences. A customer's patience bar turns orange. The player notices and adjusts.

The combo counter climbs. At ×1.5, something shifts. The decisions become faster. The routes become shorter in the mind before the feet start moving. This is flow arriving.

The emotional state at the middle of a good session: complete absorption. No thoughts outside the restaurant exist. The player is not thinking about their day. They are not thinking about anything. They are just moving, and the moving is exactly right.

---

### Climax (minute 2:00 – 2:45)

**Target emotion: High-stakes presence**

The patience values are at their shortest. Three tables may need attention simultaneously. The combo is at its highest or has already broken. This is where the memorable moments live.

The near-miss save happens here. The impossible choice happens here. The combo break at ×2.5 happens here. The last-second delivery that saves everything happens here.

The player is not in flow — flow is comfortable. This is something sharper. Every decision is visible as a decision. The wrong choice is not just possible — it is imminent, and the player knows it.

The emotional state in the climax: alert presence. Not panic — panic is undirected. This is focused, high-resolution awareness. Every table is known. Every timer is felt. The player is choosing in real time.

---

### Ending (minute 2:45 – 3:00)

**Target emotion: Earned rest**

The 30-second warning appeared. The urgency has peaked. The session is ending. The player serves the last customers with whatever calm they can recover.

The timer hits zero. The restaurant freezes. The end screen appears.

The player exhales. Not from relief — from completion. The round is done. Their score is a number they made with their hands and their judgment. Every point in it has a cause they remember.

The emotional state at the end: brief stillness, immediately followed by calculation. "What would I do differently?" The question arrives before the end screen finishes loading. This is the sound of a player about to press "PLAY AGAIN."

## 8. VISUAL SIGNATURES

What makes TableRush recognizable from a single screenshot?

Ten visual elements that, seen once, immediately identify this game.

---

**1. The top-down restaurant floor with five tables and one moving character**

The camera perspective — overhead, slightly angled — with visible tables and one character navigating between them. This composition is instantly legible as a restaurant service game.

**2. The colored priority indicator above a table**

A bold colored arrow or glow pointing down at one specific table, while other tables are visibly dimmer. The visual hierarchy of "this is where you go" communicates the entire game loop in one image.

**3. The patience pill above a customer's head**

A thin but visible bar — green fading to yellow fading to red — floating above a customer character. This is the universal language of "time is running out" combined with "a person is waiting." The patience bar is not a health bar. It is a social obligation.

**4. The warm cream restaurant floor**

The specific shade of warm cream — not a gray grid, not a blue tile — combined with the mahogany brown tables. The color temperature communicates "welcoming restaurant" not "sterile kitchen."

**5. The waiter mid-movement between two tables**

Not standing still. Not at a destination. Mid-stride. The image of purposeful motion defines the game: this character is always going somewhere specific, always with a reason.

**6. A customer's speech bubble showing a food emoji**

The food emoji in a speech bubble — warm cream fill, colored border — is a visual metaphor for "this person has made their order and is waiting." It communicates hunger, desire, and the player's obligation simultaneously.

**7. The kitchen counter in the upper third of the screen**

The kitchen is always at the top. Its presence in the upper portion of every screenshot communicates the directional flow of the game: players move up toward the kitchen and back down toward the tables. The vertical axis has meaning.

**8. Multiple customers in different states simultaneously**

One customer with a ❓ bubble, another with a food emoji bubble, another eating (calm face), another paying (💳 bubble). The density of simultaneous states communicates both complexity and readability. "There are multiple things happening and I can understand all of them."

**9. The combo counter in the HUD**

"🔥 ×2.5" in orange, visible in the top center of the screen. Even in a screenshot, the multiplier communicates that there is a consequence system — this number matters, and it is going up or down based on what just happened.

**10. The floating score pop**

"+380" floating above a table, mid-fade, in the moment after a delivery. The floating number is the visual grammar of "this action had a value." It ties action to reward so tightly that even in a still image it communicates the game's logic.

## 10. FINAL DECISION: WHAT IS TABLERRUSH?

TableRush is a three-minute performance. 

The player is a waiter in a busy restaurant. They are not the owner, not the chef, not the manager. They are the person on the floor — the one who reads the room, moves with intention, and makes every customer feel like the only person in the building. The game is not about survival. It is not about building or managing or planning. It is about the specific, repeatable pleasure of being exceptionally good at something under pressure, in public, in real time.

The loop is simple: customers arrive, orders go to the kitchen, food is picked up, delivered, payment collected, table cleared. Three minutes. When the loop is working — when the player is ahead of every demand, when the combo is climbing, when the last-second save happens and the customer sits back down — there is no better feeling available in a mobile game. Because the feeling is not a game feeling. It is a human feeling: the satisfaction of competence, executed in front of an audience, in the place you were made for.

That is what TableRush is. The rest is execution.
